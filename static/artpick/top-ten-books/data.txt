      <h2>The List of Books</h2>
<p><em><br />
  We awarded points for each selection – 10 points for a first place pick, nine points for a second place pick, and so on. Then we totaled up all the points and ranked them accordingly. Here are all the books ordered by the number of points each earned. In the parentheses are the initials of the authors that selected them and the points earned. Click on their initials to see their list. </em></p>
    </div>

        <div class="views-row views-row-1 views-row-odd views-row-first">
      
  <div class="views-field views-field-body">        <div class="field-content top-ten-book-item"><p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0679720200/ref=nosim/totebo07-20"><img src="http://toptenbooks.net/thebooks/sites/default/files/Stranger.jpg" width="74" height="123" />The Stranger</a> by Albert Camus (1942). The opening lines—“Mother died today. Or, maybe, yesterday. I can’t be sure”—epitomize Camus’s celebrated notions of “the absurd.” His narrator, Meursault, a wretched little Algerian clerk sentenced to death for the murder, feels nothing: no remorse, love, guilt, grief, or hope. But he’s not a sociopath; he’s just honest. An embodiment of existential philosophy, he believes in no higher power and accepts that we are born only to die. Our only choice is to act “as if” life has meaning and thereby gain some freedom.</p>
<p><strong>Total Points: 38 </strong>(<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Stanley-Crawford">SCraw</a> 2) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Edwidge-Danticat">ED</a> 9) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Denise-Gess">DG</a> 9) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Barry-Hannah">BH</a> 8) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Kent-Haruf">KH</a> 2) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Jim-Harrison">JH</a> 1) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Heidi-Julavits">HJ</a> 7)</p>
<p> </p>
</div>  </div>  </div>

  <div class="views-field views-field-body">        <div class="field-content top-ten-book-item"><p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0316769177/ref=nosim/totebo07-20"><img src="http://toptenbooks.net/thebooks/sites/default/files/Catcher%20in%20the%20Rye.jpg" width="83" height="123" />The Catcher in the Rye</a> by J. D. Salinger (1951). After being dismissed from another prep school, Holden Caulfield—whose slangy, intimate narration defines this novel—has a series of misadventures in Manhattan before going home for Christmas. Haunted by the death of brother Allie, he wants what he cannot have—to snare the elusive Jane Gallagher, to run away with his sister Phoebe, to “catch” innocent youths before they fall into the “phony” world of adults. A timeless voice of adolescent rage and assurance, Holden may rank highest in the pantheon of antiestablishment heroes.</p>
<p><strong>Total Points: 36 </strong>(<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/diana-abu-jaber">DAJ</a> 2) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Adam-Braver">ABrav</a> 2) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Bebe-Moore-Campbell">BMC</a> 10) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Carl-Hiaasen">CH</a> 8) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Alice-Hoffman">AH</a> 8) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Arthur-Golden">AGold</a> 6)</p>

  <div class="views-field views-field-body">        <div class="field-content top-ten-book-item"><p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0156004801/ref=nosim/totebo07-20"><img src="http://toptenbooks.net/thebooks/sites/default/files/All%20the%20King%27s%20Men.jpg" width="100" height="123" />All the King’s Men</a> by Robert Penn Warren (1946). In perhaps the most famous American political novel, Warren tracks the unsentimental education of Jack Burden, an upper-class, college-educated lackey to Willie Stark, the populist governor of Louisiana (whom Warren modeled on Huey Long). Burden spirals into self-loathing as he learns how political sausage is made, then finds a moral compass after Stark’s assassination—all told in a bleak poetry that marries Sartre and Tennessee Williams.</p>
<p><strong>Total Points: 35 </strong>(<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Judy-Budnitz">JBud</a> 8) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/James-Lee-Burke">JLB</a> 7) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Robb-Forman-Dew">RFD</a> 4) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Donald-Harington">DH</a> 6) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/George-Pelacanos">GP</a> 10)</p>

  <div class="views-field views-field-body">        <div class="field-content top-ten-book-item"><p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0156004801/ref=nosim/totebo07-20"><img src="http://toptenbooks.net/thebooks/sites/default/files/All%20the%20King%27s%20Men.jpg" width="100" height="123" />All the King’s Men</a> by Robert Penn Warren (1946). In perhaps the most famous American political novel, Warren tracks the unsentimental education of Jack Burden, an upper-class, college-educated lackey to Willie Stark, the populist governor of Louisiana (whom Warren modeled on Huey Long). Burden spirals into self-loathing as he learns how political sausage is made, then finds a moral compass after Stark’s assassination—all told in a bleak poetry that marries Sartre and Tennessee Williams.</p>
<p><strong>Total Points: 35 </strong>(<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Judy-Budnitz">JBud</a> 8) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/James-Lee-Burke">JLB</a> 7) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Robb-Forman-Dew">RFD</a> 4) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Donald-Harington">DH</a> 6) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/George-Pelacanos">GP</a> 10)</p>

  <div class="views-field views-field-body">        <div class="field-content top-ten-book-item"><p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0393924289/ref=nosim/totebo07-20"><img src="http://toptenbooks.net/thebooks/sites/default/files/Paradise%20Lost.jpg" width="78" height="123" />Paradise Lost</a> by John Milton (1667). Recasting the biblical story of Adam and Eve’s fall from grace, this epic poem details Satan’s origins, his desire for revenge, his transformation into the serpent, and his seduction of Eve. The poem extends our understanding of Christian myth in lush and challenging language. Though Milton seeks to explain “the ways of God to man,” he gives Satan— “Better to reign in hell than serve in heaven”— the best lines.</p>
<p><strong>Total Points: 33 </strong>(<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Andrea-Barrett">AB</a> 10) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Michael-Chabon">MC</a> 3) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Fred-Chappell">FC</a> 2) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Adam-Haslett">AHas</a> 9) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/siri-hustvedt">SHust</a> 9)</p>

  <div class="views-field views-field-body">        <div class="field-content top-ten-book-item"><p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0679444599/ref=nosim/totebo07-20"><img src="http://toptenbooks.net/thebooks/sites/default/files/Rabbit%2C%20Run.jpg" width="77" height="123" />Rabbit Angstrom</a>— <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0449911659/ref=nosim/totebo07-20">Rabbit, Run</a> (1960), <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0449911934/ref=nosim/totebo07-20">Rabbit Redux</a> (1971), <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0449911829/ref=nosim/totebo07-20">Rabbit Is Rich</a> (1981), <a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0449911942/ref=nosim/totebo07-20">Rabbit at Rest</a> (1990)— by John Updike. Read as four discrete stories or as a seamless quartet, the Rabbit novels are a tour de force chronicle, critique, and eloquent appreciation of the American white Protestant middle-class male and the swiftly shifting culture around him in the last four decades of the twentieth century. From his feckless youth as a promising high school athlete and unready husband and father in Rabbit, Run; through vulgar affluence, serial infidelity, and guilt as a car dealer in Rabbit Redux; to angry bewilderment over 1970s social upheaval in Rabbit Is Rich, the meaningfully named Rabbit Angstrom gamely tries to keep up with it all, to be a good guy. But the world is too much with, and for, Rabbit, who staggers through literal and metaphorical heart failure before finally falling in Rabbit at Rest.</p>
<p><strong>Total Points: 33 </strong>(<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Lee-K-Abbott">LKA</a> 5) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Julian-Barnes">JBarn</a> 2) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/bret-easton-ellis">BEE</a> 3) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/G-D-Gearino">GDG</a> 2) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/robert-goolrick">RG</a> 2) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Ken-Kalfus">KK</a> 4) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Thomas-Mallon">TM</a> 1) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Tom-Perrotta">TP</a> 2) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Roxana-Robinson">RR</a> 3) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Scott-Spencer">SS</a> 1) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Scott-Turow">ST</a> 8)</p>

  <div class="views-field views-field-body">        <div class="field-content top-ten-book-item"><p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0060931736/ref=nosim/totebo07-20"><img src="http://toptenbooks.net/thebooks/sites/default/files/Prime%20of%20Miss%20Jean%20Brodie.jpg" width="79" height="123" />The Prime of Miss Jean Brodie</a> by Muriel Spark (1961). The Miss Brodie in question is a wildly popular teacher in a 1930s Edinburgh middle school. <p>Appreciation of the Stories of Eudora Welty by <a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/newsingle.cgi?1269743073">Louis D. Rubin, Jr.</a></p>
<p>At first glance, the people Eudora Welty usually writes about seem unremarkable, as are the mostly Mississippi towns, cities, and countryside they live in.</p>
<p>To read her stories, however, such as “Why I Live at the P.O.,” “Petrified Man,” “Powerhouse,” “Moon Lake,” and “Kin,” is to learn otherwise. Through them you enter the realm of the extraordinary, as revealed in the commonplace. Each of Welty’s seemingly mundane people exhibits the depth, complexity, private surmise, and ultimate riddle of human identity. “Every-body to their own visioning,” as one of her characters remarks.</p>
<p>There is plenty of high comedy. Few authors can match her eye for the incongruous, the hilarious response, the bemused quality of the way her people go about their lives. There is also pathos, veering sometimes into tragedy, and beyond that, awareness of what is unknowable and inscrutable.</p>
<p>Her most stunning fiction is the group of interconnected stories published in 1949 as The Golden Apples. These center on the citizenry of Morgana, Mississippi, over the course of some four decades. In “June Recital” German-born Miss Lottie Elisabeth Eckhart teaches piano to the young. These include Cassie Morrison, who carries on her teacher’s mission, and Virgie Rainey, most talented of all, who in spite of herself absorbs “the Beethoven, as with the dragon’s blood.” In the closing story, “The Wanderers,” Virgie sits under a tree not far from Miss Eckhart’s grave and gazes into the falling rain, hearing “the magical percussion” drumming into her ears: “That was the gift she had touched with her fingers that had drifted and left her.”</p>
<p>Welty’s richly allusive style, alive to nuance, is not really like anyone else’s. The dialogue (her characters talk at rather than to one another), the shading of Greek mythology and W. B. <p>Opening The Charterhouse of Parma is like stepping into the path of a benevolent cyclone that will pick you up and set you down, gently but firmly, somewhere else. <p>Stendhal marks the boundaries of the more traditional nineteenth-century novel, and then proceeds to explode them. Just as Fabrizio keeps discovering that his life is taking a different direction from what he’d imagined, so the reader keeps thinking that Stendhal has written one kind of book, then finding that it is something else entirely. Stendhal writes as if he can’t see why everything—politics, history, intrigue, the battle of Waterloo, a love story, several love stories—can’t be compressed into a single novel. Fearing an invasion, the empire sends an army to eliminate the threat of neighboring “barbarians,” and the magistrate, accused of plotting with the enemy, is beaten and jailed. As he suffers these trials, the magistrate reflects on civilization and nature, suffering and oppression, and man’s barbarous tendency toward violence.</p>
<p><strong>Total Points: 20 </strong>(<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Jim-Crace">JC</a> 8) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/laila-lalami">LL</a> 10) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Jim-Shepard">JS</a> 2)</p>
</div>  </div>  </div>
  <div class="views-row views-row-44 views-row-even">
      
  <div class="views-field views-field-body">        <div class="field-content top-ten-book-item"><p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0679767924/ref=nosim/totebo07-20"><img src="http://toptenbooks.net/thebooks/sites/default/files/Independent%20People.jpg" width="77" height="123" />Independent People</a> by Halldór Laxness (1934). The Icelandic Nobel laureate’s best novel is a chronicle of endurance and survival, whose stubborn protagonist Bjartür “of Summerhouses” is a sheepherder at odds with inclement weather, poverty, society in particular and authority in general, and his own estranged family. Laxness unflinchingly dramatizes Bjartür’s unloving, combative relationships with his step-daughter Asta and frail son Nonni (a possible authorial surrogate)—yet finds the perverse heroism in this bad shepherd’s compulsive pursuit of freedom (from even the Irish sorcerer who had cursed his land). This is an antihero for whom readers will find themselves cheering.</p>
<p><strong>Total Points: 19 </strong>(<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Jonathan-Franzen">JF</a> 1) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Jim-Harrison">JH</a> 5) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Adam-Haslett">AHas</a> 4) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/David-Means">DMe</a> 9)</p>
</div>  </div>  </div>
  <div class="views-row views-row-45 views-row-odd">
      
  <div class="views-field views-field-body">        <div class="field-content top-ten-book-item"><p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/1883011744/ref=nosim/totebo07-20"><img src="http://toptenbooks.net/thebooks/sites/default/files/My%20Antonia.jpg" width="81" height="123" />My Ántonia</a> by Willa Cather (1918). Featuring a beleaguered central heroine who endures her father’s suicide, is driven to work in the fields, and is seduced, abandoned, and left pregnant, this ought to be a tale of tragic inevitability. Instead, this beautifully elegiac novel offers an unsentimental paean to the prairie, to domesticity, and to memory itself. As remembered by her friend Jim, Ántonia is as mythic and down-to-earth as the Nebraska she inhabits.</p>
<p><strong>Total Points: 19 </strong>(<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Elizabeth-Flock">EF</a> 7) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Tom-Perrotta">TP</a> 5) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Richard-Powers">RPow</a> 1) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Brad-Watson">BW</a>5) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Meg-Wolitzer">MW</a> 1)</p>
</div>  </div>  </div>
  <div class="views-row views-row-46 views-row-even">
      
  <div class="views-field views-field-body">        <div class="field-content top-ten-book-item"><p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0141186615/ref=nosim/totebo07-20"><img src="http://toptenbooks.net/thebooks/sites/default/files/Parade%27s%20End.jpg" width="77" height="123" />Parade’s End</a> by Ford Madox Ford (1928). Christopher Tietjens, “the last English Tory,” is an exemplar of the old order; his faithless wife Sylvia represents the new. Grounded in their relationship, this rueful modernist epic dissects the intricacies of Edwardian England and the forces unleashed by World War I that would, inevitably and necessarily, slay that genteel world.</p>
<p><strong>Total Points: 19 </strong>(<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Margot-Livesey">ML</a> 8) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Bobbie-Ann-Mason">BAM</a> 3) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Craig-Nova">CN</a> 8)</p>
</div>  </div>  </div>
  <div class="views-row views-row-47 views-row-odd">
      
  <div class="views-field views-field-body">        <div class="field-content top-ten-book-item"><p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0684843323/ref=nosim/totebo07-20"><img src="http://toptenbooks.net/thebooks/sites/default/files/Ernest%20Hemingway.jpg" width="82" height="123" />Stories of Ernest Hemingway</a> (1899–1961). Love and war, childhood and adolescence, and initiation and experience are recurring themes in these journalistically spare, often autobiographical stories. Some, including “A Very Short Story,” are merely snapshots, barely a page long. Others, such as Snows of Kilimanjaro” are multi-layered stories within a story. Among the best are those featuring Hemingway’s doppelganger Nick Adams, whose youthful innocence in an Edenic Michigan becomes an almost jaded stoicism through combat and failed romance.</p>
<p><strong>Total Points: 19 </strong>(<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Melissa-Bank">MB</a> 8) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Clyde-Edgerton">CE</a> 2) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Kent-Haruf">KH</a> 6) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Susan-Minot">SM</a> 3)</p>
</div>  </div>  </div>
  <div class="views-row views-row-48 views-row-even">
      
  <div class="views-field views-field-body">        <div class="field-content top-ten-book-item"><p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/1598530461/ref=nosim/totebo07-20"><img src="http://toptenbooks.net/thebooks/sites/default/files/Raymond%20Carver.jpg" width="80" height="123" />Stories of Raymond Carver</a> (1938–88). Culled from his own hard-drinking, working-class upbringing in the Pacific Northwest, Carver’s stories depict relationships in various states of decay, the unsung losses of unsung people, and the prolonged misery of ordinary people delivered in a sly understated tone sometimes called dirty realism. A master of the short story, Carver’s name was only beginning to be mentioned in the same breath as Hemingway and O’Connor when lung cancer brought him down at the age of fifty.</p>
<p><strong>Total Points: 19 </strong>(<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Adam-Braver">ABrav</a> 9) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Melissa-Bank">MB</a> 5) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Elizabeth-Flock">EF</a> 4) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Tom-Perrotta">TP</a> 1)</p>
</div>  </div>  </div>
  <div class="views-row views-row-49 views-row-odd">
      
  <div class="views-field views-field-body">        <div class="field-content top-ten-book-item"><p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0141439599/ref=nosim/totebo07-20"><img src="http://toptenbooks.net/thebooks/sites/default/files/Tess%20of%20d%27Ubervilles.jpg" width="79" height="123" />Tess of the D’Urbervilles</a> by Thomas Hardy (1891). When Tess’s mother learns that her humble family has lofty bloodlines, she sends her daughter out to cadge funds and land a rich husband. Instead Tess suffers cruel mistreatment and becomes pregnant. The baby’s death unleashes torrents of grief, guilt, and religious doubt. However, Hardy’s grim tale is lightened by his loving descriptions of the English landscape and his humorous rendering of local talk.</p>
<p><strong>Total Points: 19 </strong>(<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/jennifer-gilmore">JGil</a> 3) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/John-Irving">JI</a> 9) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Reynolds-Price">RPri</a> 7)</p>
</div>  </div>  </div>
  <div class="views-row views-row-50 views-row-even views-row-last">
      
  <div class="views-field views-field-body">        <div class="field-content top-ten-book-item"><p><a href="http://www.amazon.com/exec/obidos/ASIN/0141439831/ref=nosim/totebo07-20"><img src="http://toptenbooks.net/thebooks/sites/default/files/Vanity%20Fair.jpg" width="92" height="123" />Vanity Fair</a> by William Makepeace Thackeray (1847–48). The subtitle is “A Novel Without a Hero,” and never was a hero more unnecessary. In Becky Sharp, we find one of the most delicious heroines of all time. Sexy, resourceful, and duplicitous, Becky schemes her way through society, always with an eye toward catching a richer man. Cynical Thackeray, whose cutting portraits of society are hilarious, resists the usual punishments doled out to bad Victorian women and allows that the vain may find as much happiness in their success as the good do in their virtue.</p>
<p><strong>Total Points: 19 </strong>(<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/John-Banville">JB</a> 1) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/bret-easton-ellis">BEE</a> 2) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/Thomas-Mallon">TM</a> 10) (<a href="http://www.toptenbooks.net/authors/martha-mcphee">MMCPH</a> 6)</p>
</div>  </div>  </div>
